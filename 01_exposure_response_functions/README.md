# Documentation of the exposure response functions

## 1. Study Design & Data Scope
This analysis employs a **two-stage time series design** to estimate the non-linear relationship between summer temperature and mortality at the municipality ßlevel, aggregated to districts. The study period covers June through September from 2014 to 2024.

### Spatial Units & exclusions
- **Primary Unit:** Swiss municipalities (*Gemeinden*).
- **Aggregation Level:** Districts (*Bezirke*), nested within Cantons (*Kantone*).
- **Data Harmonization:** 
  - Mortality data sourced from federal statistical office.
  - Temperature data: Population-weighted daily mean temperatures derived from national models (meteoSwiss).
  - **Exclusion Criteria:** 141 communes were excluded because their boundaries did not align with the **2026 administrative standards** (`Gemeindestand_lookup_districts.csv`). Analyses proceed only on harmonized units matching the current district definitions.

---

## 2. Step-by-Step Analytical Workflow

### Stage 1: community-level modeling (small area estimation)
The first stage estimates exposure-response curves independently for each valid municipality using a time-stratified case-crossover approach adapted for small areas.

1.  **Data preparation:**
    - Merge mortality counts and temperature data by municipality code (`BFS Gde-nummer`) and date.
    - Filter for summer months (June–September).
    - Generate temporal covariates: Day of Year (`doy`), Day of Week (`dow`), and Month.

2.  **Cross-basis Cconstruction:**
    - **Temperature variable:** Modeled using Natural Splines (`ns`) with internal knots at the **50th and 90th percentiles** of local temperature distributions. Boundary knots set to the observed range.
    - **Lag structure:** A lag dimension of **3 days** is applied, modeled with "integer" specification.
    - **grouping:** Data is grouped by `municipality-year` combinations to allow for over-dispersion correction within strata.

3.  **Model fitting (GNM):**
    - A Generalized Non-linear Model (`gnm` with `quasipoisson` family) is fitted:
      $$ \log(E[Y_{it}]) = \beta_0 + CB(T_{it}, L) + S(DOW_t) $$
      Where $CB$ is the cross-basis term for temperature and lag, and seasonal/daily patterns are controlled via stratification (eliminating empty strata to prevent bias).
    - **Minimum Mortality Temperature (MMT):** Identified for each commune using the `findmin` function, scanning between the 25th and 90th percentiles of local temperature.

4.  **Stage 1 output extraction:**
    - The `crossreduce` function extracts the coefficient matrix ($\hat{\beta}$) and variance-covariance matrices ($V$) for the reduced cross-ba

### Stage 2: mixed-effects meta-regression & BLUPs
The second stage pools community-level estimates while accounting for spatial hierarchy and heterogeneity.

1.  **Meta-covariates generation:**
    - **District characteristics:** Mean temperature (`avgtmean`) and temperature range (`rangetmean`) calculated for each district during the summer period.
    - **Covariate alignment:** Merged with lookup tables to ensure Canton/District hierarchy is preserved.

2.  **Two-level random effects model:**
    - A mixed-meta regression (`mixmeta` package, REML method) is applied:
      $$ \hat{\beta}_{jk} = X_{jk}\gamma + u_{j[k]} + \epsilon_{jk} $$
      - **Fixed effects:** Explained by district-level temperature range and mean.
      - **Random effects:** Nested structure defined as `~1 | Kanton / Bezirks-nummer` to account for correlation among districts within cantons and among communes within districts.
    
3.  **Prediction & aggregation:**
    - **BLUPs (Best Linear Unbiased Predictors):** Extracted for every district to obtain stable, shrunken estimates that borrow strength across neighboring units.
    - **National/regional curves:** Generated by predicting coefficients at the mean national temperature characteristics, allowing for a smooth pooled exposure-response curve.

## 3. Statistical software & packages
- **Core engine:** R (v4.x+)
- **Spatial & raster handling:** `sf`, `terra`, `exactextractr` (for population-weighted extraction).
- **Epidemiological modeling:** `dlnm` (Distributed Lag Non-linear Models), `gnm` (Generalized Nonlinear Models).
- **Meta-analysis:** `mixmeta` (Multivariate Meta-Analysis).
- **Data management:** `data.table`, `dplyr`, `tidyr`.
- **Visualization:** `ggplot2`, `patchwork`.

---

## 4. Key outputs
1.  **Stage 1 plots:** Individual exposure-response curves for all included districts (visualizing local variability).
2.  **BLUP district maps/curves:** District-specific risk estimates (RED lines in output PDFs) incorporating hierarchical smoothing.
3.  **Pooled national curve:** The summary exposure-response function representing the average risk across Switzerland during summer.
4.  **Sensitivity metrics:** Identification of district-specific Minimum Mortality Temperatures (MMT) ranging typically between the 50th–90th percentiles of local summer heat.

---

## 5. Limitations & future improvements
- **Temporal resolution:** Daily mortality counts assume no significant misclassification of death dates.
- **Lag selection:** A fixed 5-day lag assumes acute/sub-acute effects; longer lags were not tested in this specific run.
- **Boundary changes:** The exclusion of 141 communes ensures administrative consistency with 2026 standards but may introduce selection bias if excluded areas have distinct climate-health dynamics.
- **Temporal biases:** Sensitivity analyses should be performed excluding the covid-19 affected summers to verify robustness of exposure response curves
- **Error BLUPs:** Error to be spotted in the variance covariance matrices of the blups (confidence intervals have a strange shape).
